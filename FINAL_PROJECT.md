# Vision Zero DC is working, kind of. #
Vision Zero DC promised to end the traffic fatalities and major injuries in the nation’s capital. Instead, the numbers are showing a different story. While making the streets safer overall, the goal is still nowhere in sight after 10 plus years. 

Vision Zero is a program that is aimed to improve pedestrian and bicycle transportation in Washington, D.C. according to the website. The program is part of Mayor Bowser’s plans to make a safer city. Vision Zero was launched in 2015. Vision Zero released the traffic safety statistics based off of the OpenDataDC datasets. Vision Zero lists on their website that all data comes from the MPD (Metropolitan Police Department).

German Virgil, Public Information Officer for DDOT (District Department of Transportation), said that all information on Vision Zero DC can be found on their website.

Vision Zero uses a “safe system approach.” According to the website, this approach has five main agencies that it follows: Safe Users, Safe Streets, Safe Vehicles, Safe Speeds, and Post Crash Care. 

Safe Users refers to all on the roadways, such as bikers, drivers, pedestrians, bus passengers, and more. Safe Streets aims to control the roads through street signs and designated driving times. Safe Vehicles indicates the focus on regulations on the vehicles within the district. Safe Speeds has helped lower the speeds throughout the district. Finally, Post Crash Care is the response of first responders as well as the forensic analyses.

“The goal of Vision Zero is to eliminate all fatal and serious injury crashes,” a direct quote from the Vision Zero website. Although the program has been active in D.C. for a little over 10 years, the progress appears to be somewhat minimal, especially towards the goal of the program. 

The data below identified is focused on fatalities and major injuries per ward in the years of 2016 and 2017. The first chart displays the data from the Vision Zero website, while the second chart displays the data directly from the OpenDataDC website. 
  
The raw data and the data presented by Vision Zero do not match. The differences are very slim. These charts show two years after the initial launch of the program in 2015. Although there is this discrepancy, Ward 2 saw the largest drop between 2016 and 2017. Both Ward 1 and Ward 3 remain with the smallest difference between the two years. 

Overall, the difference between those two years is not significant enough to confidently say that Vision Zero was effective. 

Looking at two of the most recent years, 2024 and 2025, the difference is staggering. For each ward, the fatalities and major injuries dropped since the 2016 and 2017 years of data. 
  
The data shows traffic safety has been improved. Although, the raw data and the data presented from Vision Zero, are still not consistent with each other. 

The data agrees that fatalities and major injuries from traffic accidents has dropped, but these major accidents still occur. 

Although there are drops in the fatalities and major injuries traffic accidents, the data does not solely support that the cause is from Vision Zero DC, especially considering the pandemic. 

Ellen Tannor, a long-time driver in D.C., does not believe the reduction of fatal and major injury traffic accidents is due to Vision Zero DC but rather the pandemic. 

“I know people think traffic is still pretty bad in DC, which it is, but it is nothing compared to what it was before the pandemic, and I don't think it'll ever kind of reached that full scale again,” Tannor said. 

Tannor said that with the new telework options, less and less people now come into the city, which she believes reduces the likelihood of traffic accidents. 

“I remember, you know, years before the pandemic when I would go into the city every single day, you know, because there were no remote options, and just how long it would take, and like, you know, you could bet a couple of days out of a week, there was going to be an accident that had traffic backed up, right, from your door out of your house almost,” Tannor said. 

With the less people now, Tannor said it is rare to see a bad traffic day due to a traffic accident. Tannor said that she knows the city has taken several measures to reduce fatal and major injury traffic accidents, but she cannot attribute the drop in accidents to the city. 

Tannor has said she has noticed more road signs such as the “no turn on red.” She has even gotten caught by the speed cameras.

“Last year, my husband had to have a sit down with me,” Tannor said, “He's like, you're probably at $1000 worth of tickets now.”

Tannor said she got three tickets all at the same place and within the same week. Tannor said the residential areas have much stricter speed limits. Although Tannor has received those three tickets, she said she prefers that method over getting pulled over. 

“At the end of the day, a ticket in the mail, you can't get into an unnecessary confrontation,” Tannor said. 

In spite of all the preventions, fatal and major injury accidents still occur. On February 13, 2026, around 2:00 p.m., a trash truck hit two people who were riding an e-bike. Following the hit, the truck then drove off and was later found in Maryland. 

According to MPD press release, the operator of the e-bike died at the scene while the passenger was taken to the hospital in critical condition. 

With accidents such as this, residents voice their frustrations and dislike of Vision Zero on the social platform Reddit. Some even changing the name to “Zero Vision.” 
 
Even with the success of Vision Zero DC, residents still believe more needs done. The initial goal is nowhere in sight, with fatal accidents such as this still occurring.
